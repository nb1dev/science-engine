Section 3 · The Story Behind Your Results

Why your gut looks this way

This isn't random — there's a sequence of events that led here. Understanding the cascade helps you address root causes, not just symptoms.

The short story — a four-stage cascade

Stage 1: Insufficient dietary fiber reduced the competitive advantage of fiber-degrading bacteria. At 13.1%, they've lost their dominant position but retain moderate species diversity — the decline is ongoing, not catastrophic. Stage 2: As fiber degraders contracted, Akkermansia muciniphila expanded into the vacated ecological space — using your gut's own mucin as fuel, an endogenous substrate that's always available regardless of diet. Stage 3: Butyrate producers depend on cross-feeding intermediates from fiber degraders. With fiber degraders diminished, the substrate pipeline dried up — even though the butyrate-producing species themselves are still present and well-diversified. Stage 4: The ecosystem reached a metastable state — mucin degradation partially compensating, health markers still positive, but structural integrity eroding. The good news: this cascade is reversible. Your protocol directly targets all three stages simultaneously.

Understanding your report

Key terms explained

📖 Glossary — key terms used in this report
Butyrate
A short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) produced when bacteria ferment fiber. It's the primary fuel for your gut lining cells and has anti-inflammatory properties.
SCFA / Short-Chain Fatty Acids
Beneficial compounds produced by bacteria fermenting fiber. Includes butyrate, acetate, and propionate. These nourish your gut, regulate immunity, and influence mood.
Shannon Diversity
A scientific measure of species diversity. A higher score means more variety of different bacterial species — a more resilient, adaptable gut ecosystem.
Guild
A group of bacteria that share a similar ecological function — like a specialist team. There are six guilds in this report, each with a different job in your gut.
Dysbiosis
An imbalance in gut bacteria — when harmful species overgrow or beneficial species are depleted. The opposite of a healthy, balanced microbiome.
Akkermansia muciniphila
